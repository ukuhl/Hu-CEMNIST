Welcome to the Hu-CEMNIST study!

Thank you for taking the time to participate in our research.
With this study, we want to understand how people visually explain what makes one digit different from another. Your participation is invaluable and will contribute to a better understanding of human decision-making.
This research project is conducted by Dr. Ulrike Kuhl at Bielefeld University. The study was approved by the Ethics Committee of Bielefeld University, Germany. People of any gender, aged 18 and above, are invited to participate.

What to Expect

The study will take approximately 10 minutes to complete.
You will be shown a symbol and asked to highlight specific areas that make this symbol look like a specified "target" symbol instead of the original one.
For example, you may be asked to highlight areas that make a "9" look more like a "0".
Afterwards, you will be asked to answer a short questionnaire assessing your experience with the system.

Your Rights as a Participant

Your participation in this research study is voluntary.
You may choose not to participate. If you decide to participate, you may withdraw at any time without giving any reason. In this case, all data collected will be deleted.
Your responses will be treated confidentially. No identifying information such as your name, email address or IP address will be recorded.
After completion of the study, the research data collected will be made publicly available in anonymized form that does not allow any conclusions about individual or specific persons. The purpose, type, and scope of potential subsequent use are currently not foreseeable.

Contact Information

If you have any questions or comments about the research study, please contact Dr. Ulrike Kuhl at ukuhl@techfak.uni-bielefeld.de.

Debriefing: Why This Research is Important

Our goal was to learn how people visually show the differences between two digits (for example, how “0” differs from “9”). By highlighting parts of the image that make it look more like one digit than another, we collected insights into how people intuitively distinguish between different digits.

These insights will:

  • Help researchers create and check computer programs that explain how they recognize digits.
  • Serve as a guide to compare human-based explanations with computer-generated ones.
  • Advance research on making computer algorithms clearer and more understandable.
